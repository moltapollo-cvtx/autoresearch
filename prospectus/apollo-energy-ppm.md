## 3. Executive Summary

### Overview

Apollo Energy Resources LLC is a five-division energy services platform raising **$1,500,000** in equity to accelerate growth across existing operating and prospective customer relationships that include **SpaceX, Blue Origin, Microsoft Houston, Uber/Waymo Austin, and active mining operations**. The equity raise is paired with **$1,500,000** of concurrent term debt financing, providing a total capital injection of **$3,000,000** to support equipment acquisition, an Installations-division acquisition, and working capital.

Apollo operates from **17311 Market St, Channelview, TX 77530** under active federal motor carrier authority, with **USDOT 4045882** and **MC-1532033**. The Company's status is **Active**, with a **Satisfactory safety rating (06/2024)**. Apollo currently operates a fleet of **30 power units with 30 drivers**, transporting **Liquids/Gases and Chemicals**, and conducts business through five integrated divisions: **Logistics, Field Services, Fleet Services, Installations, and Generator Maintenance**. The Company also generates revenue through a Brokerage activity, which provides pass-through logistics coordination.

Apollo is not presenting a pre-revenue concept. The Company generated **$4.9M in FY2025A revenue** (Apollo standalone, excluding the Installations acquisition target) and is deploying capital into existing customer relationships, required equipment, and capacity expansion intended to convert current operating demand into contracted, multi-division revenue where commercially achievable.

### Current Operating Relationships and Contracting Objective

Apollo distinguishes between: (i) customers and counterparties with whom it has active operating relationships today, and (ii) Apollo's six-month objective of securing one contracted anchor customer per division.

- **Active operating relationships today:** **SpaceX and Blue Origin** (fuel logistics activity in Texas), **Microsoft** (subcontractor activity in Houston), **Uber / Waymo** (subcontractor activity in Austin), and an **active mining operation out west**.
- **Pipeline / prospective opportunities:** **two additional liquefaction-compression (L-C) sites**.
- **Six-month objective:** one contracted anchor customer for each of Apollo's five divisions, with duration beyond six months, established pricing, and reduced rate volatility.

Accordingly, investors should not read the customer list above as meaning that all such relationships are presently governed by long-term contracts with guaranteed minimum volumes.

### Investment Highlights

- **Operating platform with recognized customers and counterparties.** Apollo currently services or supports operating customer relationships that include **SpaceX and Blue Origin as a principal fuel logistics provider in Texas**, **Microsoft as a subcontractor in Houston**, **Uber/Waymo as a subcontractor in Austin**, an **active mining operation out west**, and **two additional liquefaction-compression sites in pipeline**.
- **Five-division structure with Brokerage overlay.** The Company is organized to serve adjacent energy and industrial service needs across logistics, field support, maintenance, installations, and equipment service, supplemented by a brokerage revenue line providing pass-through logistics coordination.
- **Installations acquisition.** Apollo intends to acquire an established installations business with approximately **$1.8M in trailing revenue**, adding immediate scale and technical capability to its gas piping division.
- **Contract-first operating plan.** Apollo's six-month objective is one contracted anchor customer per division, each with duration beyond six months, established pricing, and reduced rate volatility.
- **NAICS-benchmarked operating discipline.** Apollo tracks margin expectations by vertical against relevant NAICS reference points as a discipline for underwriting, pricing, and operating performance.
- **Regulatory and technical barriers.** Operations depend on safety compliance, certifications, specialized equipment, and domain expertise that limit commoditization.
- **Defined capital deployment plan.** The **$1,500,000** equity raise, paired with **$1,500,000** of term debt financing, supports a **15-month runway** focused on nitrogen trailers, work trucks, tools/equipment, ISO tanks, working capital, and the Installations acquisition.
- **Founder retention of control.** Management is expected to retain **at least 85% post-close** if the full **$1,500,000** offering is sold at the stated valuation.
- **Aggressive deleveraging.** The Company's model projects total debt declining from **$4.6M in FY2026** to **$477K by FY2030**, creating a net cash position at exit.

## 4. Risk Factors

An investment in the Units offered hereby involves a high degree of risk. Prospective investors should carefully consider the following risk factors, together with the other information contained in this Memorandum, before making an investment decision. The risks described below are not the only risks facing the Company. Additional risks and uncertainties, including risks not presently known to the Company or that the Company currently deems immaterial, may also impair the Company's business, financial condition, results of operations, cash flows, and the value of the Units. The occurrence of any of the following events could cause an investor to lose all or a substantial portion of its investment.

### Customer Concentration and Relationship-Form Risk

Apollo's near-term revenue base is expected to be concentrated among a limited number of commercial and industrial counterparties and project channels. The Company's customer set includes large counterparties and project environments with established vendor qualification processes, purchase order systems, and formal jobsite compliance requirements; however, the legal form of such customer relationships may vary materially. Certain relationships may be governed by master service agreements, but others may be evidenced by purchase orders, dispatches, work orders, subcontract assignments, recurring field call-outs, or other course-of-dealing arrangements rather than long-form contracts with fixed minimum volumes. As a result, the existence of an active operating relationship does not necessarily equate to legally committed future revenue. If a significant customer reduces dispatch activity, changes procurement channels, delays site work, re-bids a program, or terminates an informal arrangement, the Company could experience a material decline in revenue, utilization, and cash flow.

### Subcontractor Status Risk

A portion of Apollo's current and anticipated work is performed on a subcontract basis rather than as prime contractor. This is particularly relevant to work associated with Microsoft Houston and Uber/Waymo Austin, where Apollo's role is through project channels that may place another contractor or integrator between Apollo and the end customer. Subcontractor status can reduce the Company's control over scope definition, scheduling, change-order recovery, invoicing cadence, payment timing, dispute resolution, and customer access. A subcontractor may also bear schedule compression, rework exposure, or collection risk without enjoying the same contractual protections as a prime contractor. Accordingly, even where end-customer demand remains healthy, Apollo may face reduced visibility and lower margin stability if intermediary counterparties alter the workstream.

### Installations Acquisition Risk

Apollo intends to acquire an existing installations business with approximately **$1.8M in trailing revenue** for a total consideration of approximately **$2.0M** ($600K cash at close plus $1.4M in seller-financed debt), supplemented by $300K in bank acquisition financing. The acquisition involves integration risk, including the retention of key personnel, customer relationships, and operating processes of the target. The Company may not realize expected synergies, and the acquired business may underperform relative to its trailing results. Goodwill and intangible assets arising from the acquisition will be carried on the Company's balance sheet. If the acquired business fails to perform, the Company may be required to recognize impairment charges and will continue to bear the debt service obligations associated with the acquisition financing regardless of operating performance.

### Leverage and Debt Service Risk

Upon closing of this Offering and the concurrent debt financing, the Company expects to have total indebtedness of approximately **$4.6M** in FY2026, consisting of existing equipment financing, new term debt, revolving credit, ISO tank financing, and acquisition-related seller and bank notes. The Company's model assumes aggressive deleveraging from **$4.6M to $477K** by FY2030; however, this trajectory depends on revenue growth, margin expansion, and disciplined capital deployment that may not materialize. Debt service obligations will consume cash flow that would otherwise be available for operations, distributions, or growth investment. A 24-month covenant holiday has been assumed, but covenant compliance thereafter depends on achieving projected EBITDA levels. If the Company fails to meet covenant thresholds after the holiday period, lenders may accelerate repayment, restrict distributions, or impose other remedies.

### Partial-Raise and No-Minimum-Offering Risk

The Company is seeking to raise $1,500,000 in this Offering but is not subject to a minimum offering threshold and may close on subscriptions in one or more closings before the full amount is raised. If the Company raises materially less than the target amount, management may be required to defer equipment purchases, reduce initial staffing plans, rely more heavily on leased or third-party assets, sequence divisional expansion more slowly than anticipated, or seek additional capital earlier than planned.

### Working Capital Timing and Cash Conversion Risk

Apollo's operations require the upfront funding of payroll, fuel, insurance, mobilization costs, repairs, subcontractors, parts, consumables, and customer-specific compliance activity, often in advance of cash collection. The Company's model assumes DSO declining from **36 to 33 days** and DPO of **18 days**, implying a net working capital gap of **18 to 15 days**. If receivables convert more slowly than projected, Apollo may need to obtain additional financing sooner than anticipated.

### Multi-Division Execution Risk

Apollo operates, or intends to operate, across five divisions: Logistics, Field Services, Fleet Services, Installations, and Generator Maintenance, with additional Brokerage revenue. While this structure is intended to diversify revenue and create cross-selling opportunities, it also increases managerial complexity. The Company must allocate capital, labor, compliance resources, equipment, and supervisory attention across transportation, field execution, shop activity, piping work, and generator service. Weakness in any one division may consume working capital and management attention disproportionate to its size. There can be no assurance that Apollo will scale each division on the timetable or at the margins contemplated by management, or that cross-division synergies will offset the complexity of a multi-line platform.

### Equipment Uptime, Fleet Age, and Maintenance Backlog Risk

The Company currently reports a fleet of 30 power units and 30 drivers. Revenue generation in Logistics and Field Services depends heavily on equipment uptime, dispatch efficiency, driver availability, inspection readiness, and preventive maintenance discipline. Specialized trailers, cryogenic equipment, service trucks, and tank-related assets may require maintenance that is more frequent, more expensive, or more technically demanding than standard over-the-road equipment. A sustained period of equipment unavailability could impair service quality, reduce revenue, and strain customer relationships.

### Safety, Hazmat, Cryogenic, and Industrial Incident Risk

Apollo transports liquids, gases, and chemicals and performs services involving LNG, nitrogen, CO₂, argon, industrial gas systems, cryogenic equipment, and generator-related field activity. These operations present inherent risks, including rollover, collision, over-pressurization, equipment failure, leak or release events, fire, explosion, environmental contamination, property damage, personal injury, and death. Apollo is an active carrier with USDOT 4045882 and MC MC-1532033 and held a Satisfactory safety rating as of June 2024; however, no safety rating or internal procedure can eliminate operating risk. A material incident could lead to litigation, regulatory action, customer loss, reputational harm, uninsured loss, increased insurance premiums, equipment grounding, or suspension of operating authority.

### Environmental Remediation Cost Risk

Apollo's transportation and field operations may involve substances, systems, and operating environments that carry environmental exposure, including cryogenic liquids, industrial gases, chemicals, fuel systems, and customer-site mechanical work. A spill, release, contamination event, or site incident could trigger cleanup obligations, third-party claims, agency response, disposal costs, site restoration obligations, or contractual indemnity exposure.

### Key Person Dependence and Absence of CFO / Controller-Level Oversight

Apollo's business is closely tied to a small group of founders and senior operating personnel, particularly Walker Anderson, Cody Graham, Cody Pate, and Philip Martin. Their customer relationships, operating experience, compliance oversight, field credibility, and trade expertise are material to the Company's current and planned operations. The Company does not currently employ a full-time chief financial officer or controller-level internal finance executive. While management intends to strengthen accounting and reporting capability as the business scales, the current absence of dedicated senior financial infrastructure increases the risk of forecasting error, working capital mismanagement, weaker internal controls, delayed reporting, and insufficient financial analysis during a period of growth.

### Regulatory, Certification, and Qualification Risk

Apollo's ability to operate and compete depends on maintaining applicable operating authority, insurance, safety compliance, customer prequalification status, and technical credentials. These may include DOT and FMCSA requirements, hazmat-related compliance, OSHA obligations, customer safety programs, welding qualifications, inspection records, pressure-testing protocols, and service-line-specific certifications. A lapse in qualification, documentation, inspection readiness, or customer-approved vendor status could interrupt operations or disqualify Apollo from attractive workstreams.

### Texas and Gulf Coast Geographic Concentration Risk

Although Apollo has active or developing work outside Texas, the Company's operational base is in Channelview, Texas, and a substantial portion of current activity is concentrated in Texas and the Gulf Coast corridor. Geographic concentration exposes Apollo to regional economic slowdowns, weather events, Gulf Coast industrial outages, local labor market tightness, regional insurance cost pressure, and state-specific regulatory or litigation dynamics.

### Customer Documentation and Revenue Durability Risk

The prominence of certain customer names in this Memorandum should not be interpreted to mean that each relationship is supported by identical documentation, identical economics, or identical duration. Some revenue may arise from recurring work orders, dispatch-based activity, or subcontract purchase orders rather than minimum-volume contracts.

### Brokerage Revenue Risk

A portion of the Company's revenue (approximately **$1.2M in FY2026**) is derived from brokerage activity — pass-through logistics coordination at approximately **15% gross margin**. Brokerage revenue is inherently lower-margin, dependent on third-party capacity, and may be subject to competitive displacement, customer direct-sourcing, or margin compression. Because brokerage revenue is included in consolidated figures, investors should understand that not all reported revenue carries the same margin profile as Apollo's core operating divisions.

### Counterparty Payment Concentration and Receivable Aging Risk

Because Apollo expects a meaningful portion of revenue to come from a limited number of counterparties or intermediaries, accounts receivable may also be concentrated. A payment dispute, delayed approval, retention practice, invoice coding issue, or financial stress event affecting one material customer could have a disproportionate effect on cash flow.

### Commodity-Linked Demand Volatility

Certain elements of Apollo's Logistics and Field Services activity are tied to industrial gas demand, LNG-related activity, launch-support operations, mining demand, and broader industrial production. These end markets can be affected by commodity prices, plant operating levels, energy cycles, customer capex decisions, and general economic conditions.

### Capital-Intensive Operating Model Risk

Apollo's operating plan requires investment in nitrogen trailers, ISO tanks, work trucks, tools, shop equipment, and related operating infrastructure. The Company's growth strategy contemplates broader capital expenditure of approximately **$4.5 million to $5.0 million** over 18–24 months; this Offering represents only the first stage of that capital plan. The Company's total FY2026 capex is projected at **$1.15M**, including both growth and maintenance capital.

### Insurance Cost and Coverage Risk

The transportation and industrial service sectors can experience elevated and volatile insurance pricing, particularly for auto liability, workers' compensation, general liability, umbrella coverage, and pollution-related exposures. The Company's model assumes total insurance expense of approximately **$625K in FY2026**, escalating at approximately 5% annually. Actual insurance costs may exceed these assumptions.

### Labor Availability, Retention, and Wage Inflation Risk

Apollo's business depends on drivers, mechanics, welders, technicians, and field personnel with relevant certifications and practical experience. Competition for such personnel in Texas and other industrial corridors can be significant.

### Competition Risk

Apollo competes with local and regional carriers, industrial gas logistics providers, field-service contractors, mechanical contractors, tank shops, and generator service companies. Some competitors are larger, better capitalized, more geographically diversified, or more deeply integrated with OEMs or prime contractors.

### Related-Party Transactions Risk

Because Apollo is a closely held founder-managed business, there is a risk that certain Founding Members may now or in the future have economic interests in entities that lease equipment to, provide services to, or otherwise transact with the Company.

### General Private Investment Risks

The Units are being offered in a private placement exempt from registration under federal and applicable state securities laws. There is no public market for the Units, and none is expected to develop. Investors should be prepared to hold their investment for an indefinite period and may have limited ability to transfer their Units.

Following the Offering, management and existing holders will retain **85.0%** of the Company's equity on a post-close basis. Accordingly, investors will hold a minority interest and will have limited ability to influence operating decisions, financing strategy, distributions, or the timing and structure of any future liquidity event.

The financial and operating information included in this Memorandum, including projections and historical summaries, is unaudited and prepared by management. No assurance can be given that the Company will achieve projected results, that investors will receive distributions, or that any liquidity event will occur at a valuation sufficient to produce an acceptable return.

## 7. Business Description

### Overview

Apollo Energy Resources LLC is a Texas-based energy services company headquartered at **17311 Market St, Channelview, TX 77530**. The Company is an active motor carrier with **USDOT 4045882** and **MC: MC-1532033**, and held a **Satisfactory safety rating as of June 2024**. The Company's business is organized into five operating divisions: **Logistics, Field Services, Fleet Services, Installations, and Generator Maintenance**, with additional revenue from **Brokerage** activity.

Apollo is not presented in this Memorandum as a pre-revenue concept. ### What Management Means by "Institutional-Grade" or Comparable Counterparties

Where this Memorandum refers to "institutional-grade" or comparable commercial and industrial counterparties, it refers to customers or project environments that typically involve formal vendor onboarding, jobsite safety requirements, purchase-order or procurement systems, invoicing discipline, and material consequences for service failure. The phrase is intended to describe customer operating context and procurement rigor, not to imply that every customer relationship is governed by the same long-form contractual structure or minimum-volume commitment.

As of the date of this Memorandum, Apollo is actively generating revenue in **Logistics** and **Field Services**. ### Practical Barriers to Entry

Management believes Apollo's operating markets contain practical barriers to entry, including:

1. **DOT and motor carrier operating authority**, including active USDOT and MC registration and related compliance obligations;
2. **Hazmat, safety, and insurance requirements** associated with transporting liquids/gases and chemicals;
3. **Customer prequalification and vendor onboarding**, which in certain industrial and aerospace-adjacent environments may require months of qualification, safety documentation, and site-specific approval;
4. **Technical trade competence**, including cryogenic handling knowledge, tank and chassis maintenance experience, industrial gas piping capability, and generator service proficiency; and
5. **Execution credibility**, particularly where customers require dependable field performance, dispatch responsiveness, and incident-free operating history.

### Cross-Division Operating Model

Apollo's division structure is intended to allow one customer relationship to support multiple service lines:

- **Logistics** creates recurring touchpoints and physical presence at customer sites.
- **Field Services** expands the value of that presence into operational support work.
- **Fleet Services** supports internal asset uptime and creates external shop revenue.
- **Installations** converts field access into fixed-site mechanical work.
- **Generator Maintenance** adds recurring service opportunities and stronger margin contribution over time.
- **Brokerage** generates supplemental revenue from logistics coordination without significant capital commitment.

### Growth Plan

Apollo's growth plan is staged and capital-led:

1. **Six Months:** Establish one contracted anchor customer in each of the five divisions, with contract duration beyond six months and established pricing. Close the Installations acquisition.
2. **18–24 Months:** Deploy approximately **$4.5 million to $5.0 million** of total capital across equipment, vehicles, facilities, and working capital and establish a second operating region based on customer demand.
3. **Five Years:** Build the platform into a larger regional and potentially multi-regional energy services business with increased contribution from higher-margin service lines. Achieve projected EBITDA of **$4.3M** and net cash position.

Management expects region selection to follow customer access rather than fixed geographic preference, with potential expansion candidates including the Permian Basin, California, the Pacific Northwest, Appalachia, a Gulf Coast extension, or the Mid-Atlantic.
