## Six-Month Vision

**One anchor customer per division. Contracted. Predictable.**

By month six, the goal is to have a contracted anchor customer for each of the five divisions. "Anchor" means:
- Contracted beyond six months (not a spot relationship)
- Rates are established — we know what we're billing and what we're earning
- No sudden margin pressure — rate risk is neutralized through contract structure

This is a foundation-setting phase. The objective is not scale — it's certainty. Six months from now, Apollo should know exactly what its baseline revenue looks like, what each division costs to run, and where the margin is. That knowledge is the prerequisite for everything that follows.

## 18–24 Month Vision

**Regional expansion. Capital deployment. Operational maturity.**

By 18 to 24 months, the targets are:

- **Total capital expenditure: $4.5–5 million.** This is the investment needed to be a real operator across all five divisions — equipment, facility, vehicles, working capital.
- **Second region established.** Either west (Permian Basin, California, Pacific Northwest) or east (Appalachia, Gulf Coast extension, Mid-Atlantic). Opportunities exist in both directions. The decision will follow where anchor customers open doors.
- **NAICS-benchmarked margins.** Each division must be operating at margins consistent with its relevant industry. Numbers should not get out of whack with public comparables and RMA data.

## 5-Year Vision

**Capital working at full velocity. Market position established. Returns compounding.**

By year five, the CapEx dollars deployed in years one through two have been converted into cash-generating machines. The return profile:

- **18–23% cash-on-cash return** on invested capital — the original $4.5–5M deployment is producing at institutional-grade yield
- **Equity value has compounded** — investment returns are not linear but explosive, reflecting the operating leverage of a multi-division platform at scale
- **20–30% market share** in each relevant market, nationwide

This is the exit-worthy version of Apollo — not just a regional operator but a platform with demonstrable market penetration across five energy services verticals. At 20–30% market capture nationwide, Apollo is no longer a small business. It's a category leader.

### Why Those Numbers Are Achievable

The math works because energy services is fragmented at the small end and sticky at the customer level. A contracted anchor customer at month six becomes a 5-year account generating predictable cash. Each division has regulatory moats (R-stamp, DOT, ASME, OEM certifications) that prevent commoditization. And the five-division structure creates cross-sell — a logistics customer is a generator maintenance prospect is a gas piping installation candidate.

The 18–23% cash-on-cash target is aggressive but grounded: generator maintenance at 42–55% gross margins with recurring contract revenue is the engine. LNG/logistics runs thinner but adds revenue scale. Tank shop and gas piping add margin stability. The blended platform, properly run, should comfortably exceed 18% cash-on-cash at maturity.

**This is the pitch:** Apollo is not a startup. It is a deliberate, NAICS-benchmarked build of a five-division energy services platform — capitalized correctly in years one and two, contracted correctly by month six, and positioned to return institutional-grade yields by year five.

## NAICS Benchmarking Framework

A core discipline of the Apollo business model: **our financial performance should track NAICS industry benchmarks for each relevant vertical.** This is not a ceiling — it is a sanity check. If a division is underperforming its industry median, we need to know why. If it's outperforming, we need to understand the driver.

### Key Benchmarking Sources (Established)
- Generator: Generac Holdings (GNRC) investor filings, EGSA member data, ServiceTitan HVAC benchmarks
- LNG: Stabilis Solutions (SLNG) FY2024 — $73M revenue, 29% gross margin, 16.1% adj. EBITDA, 6.3% net
- Tank Shop: Fullbay "State of Heavy-Duty Repair" 2024–2025 (1,000+ shops), CCJ Digital
- Gas Piping: CFMA 2024 Financial Benchmarker, EMCOR Group (EME), Comfort Systems USA (FIX)

### The Structural Reality by Division

**Generator Maintenance** is the highest-margin, most recurring division. Emergency repair hits 55–65% gross margins. Maintenance contracts at 35–45%. Revenue per tech: $200–350K. This is the anchor of the portfolio.

**LNG/Logistics** is capital-intensive and margin-thin. Commodity passthrough dominates cost structure (38–52% of revenue is LNG purchase cost). Stabilis's 29% gross margin is achievable only with owned liquefaction — without it, expect 20–25%. Contract book (vs. spot) is the primary lever: Stabilis moved from 43% to 68% contracted in one year, driving margin and predictability.

**Tank Shop (Fleet Services)** enjoys regulatory moats — DOT inspection cycles are mandatory, so demand is floor-protected. R-stamp welding commands $150–200+/hour. Key risks: insurance burden (5–9% of revenue including pollution liability), parts margin thin at 21% median (Fullbay), and customer concentration (3–10 fleet accounts often = 50–80% of revenue).

**Gas Piping (Installations)** is the lowest CapEx entry point ($150–500K startup) with certification-protected margins. Workers' comp is the silent killer at $5–15 per $100 of payroll — WC alone can consume 3–6% of revenue. Medical gas (ASSE 6010/6020/6040) and brewery CO₂ work carry the best margins. Build relationship with Airgas/Linde/Air Liquide reps — they're the primary referral channel.
